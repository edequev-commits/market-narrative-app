Asunto: Positive thinking

Plus, the economics of $60-plus cigarettes

Econ World

Econ World

By Carmel Crimmins, Host, Reuters Econ World

Hello there,

Global stocks are at record highs as investors bet we are over the worst in the Middle East. Are they being overly optimistic about the prospects for peace ? Who knows, but it feels like part of a broader theme of positive thinking on geopolitical risk.

Look at the International Monetary Fund. Yes, it cut its growth outlook but it’s still assuming a short-lived war in Iran and oil prices normalizing in the second half of 2026, with an $82 per-barrel average for the year - well below today’s benchmark Brent crude futures price of around $96.00.

Corporate earnings forecasts have hardly been affected by the oil shock, with 2027 growth forecasts at 18% and 11% for U.S. and European blue chips, respectively. As my colleague Mike Dolan points out, the temptation to screen out the war, bet on eventual de-escalation and focus on the year ahead has been hard to pass up, particularly with AI continuing to boost demand.

But what if the market’s compass is wrong? The price of a physical barrel of oil is trading at record highs while futures benchmarks signal calm. In practical terms, that means the real cost of crude for refiners, particularly in Asia, is far closer to $120 a barrel - about 65% above pre-war levels and ⁠the highest since 2022 -- than the $96 suggested by headline benchmarks. That means inflationary pressure is building up in the system.
The futures price reflects optimism about the war ending, while the physical price reflects the reality on the ground - that Middle East oil production and exports will take months, if not years, to recover to pre-war levels, even if the conflict ends now.

As my colleague Ron Bousso points out, if oil supplies currently being priced into the futures market fail to materialise by early summer - the peak demand period in the Northern Hemisphere - that could have broad consequences for the global economy.

While we wait to see how the geopolitics develop, China’s economy put on a better-than-expected performance in the first quarter, riding an export surge before the Iran war sent energy costs soaring. Britain too enjoyed a burst of growth in February, suggesting it was in slightly better shape before the start of the Iran war than many economists had feared. But the joy may be short-lived: Britain suffered the sharpest cut to economic growth forecasts for large rich economies by the IMF, due largely to the conflict.
