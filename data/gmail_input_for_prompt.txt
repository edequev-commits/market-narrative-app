Iran

• What bulls are saying : media reports suggest the US and Iran remain in communication and are working to narrow their differences , while another face-to-face meeting could occur within days . Trump told Fox’s Maria Bartiromo in an interview airing Wed morning that the war is “very close to being over” and mentioned to ABC News that “ I think you’re going to be watching an amazing two days ahead ” while saying in a NY Post interview that additional peace negotiations “could be happening over the next two days” . It appears that Trump has given up on kinetic escalation as a viable option. A Bloomberg report said Iran could suspend Hormuz shipments for the time being to avoid risking a military confrontation with the US blockade. Europe is putting together a plan that would see it commit military resources to clearing Hormuz of mines once fighting formally ends according to the WSJ . Israel and Lebanon have a long way to go before the two sides reach a deal, but the fact they are even sitting down (as they did on Tuesday) is significant (the US State Dept. issued a statement Tues afternoon and expressed hope that the current dialogue could eventually yield an agreement ).
• What bears are saying : Trump remains stuck in the Middle East , unable to escalate or exit . The bid-ask between Washington and Tehran still seems wide (20 vs. 5 years on enrichment suspension is a big gap), and time is of the essence with the ceasefire set to expire next week. A Washington Post report Wed morning talked about how the Pentagon is moving thousands of additional troops into the Middle East . It’s not just Iran – the Lebanon war continues to rage and resolving that will be even harder than reconciling the nuclear differences between Washington and Tehran. The economic fallout from the war is only just starting to seep into the data , and investors should be prepared for months (at least) of stagflationary pressure (the Mar PPI wasn’t as hot as feared, but headline still accelerated meaningfully vs. Feb to +4% Y/Y).

### **Mastering Auto Financing – How Insurance Requirements Shape Your Vehicle Loan**

When you finance a car, truck, or SUV, the lender technically owns the vehicle until the loan is paid off. This is why almost every auto loan agreement requires you to carry comprehensive and collision coverage. Skipping it can trigger immediate default, forced lender-placed insurance (which is far more expensive), or even repossession. Understanding this upfront prevents nasty surprises in your monthly budget.

**How Insurance Premiums Directly Affect Your Monthly Payment**

Lenders often calculate your debt-to-income ratio using the total cost of ownership—including insurance. A high-risk driver with expensive premiums may qualify for a smaller loan amount or face a higher interest rate. Smart buyers shop insurance quotes before visiting the dealership so they can factor realistic monthly costs into their financing decision and avoid stretching their budget too thin.

**Bundling Finance and Insurance for Maximum Savings**

Many credit unions and captive finance companies now offer “payment protection bundles” that combine loan payments with insurance premiums in one easy deduction. When structured correctly, these bundles can lower your overall interest rate by 0.25–0.75% and simplify cash flow. Always compare the total cost of ownership (loan + insurance + taxes + maintenance) rather than focusing only on the sticker price or monthly payment

It’s slowly becoming the consensus that the war (at least the military component of it) has peaked , with a sustained détente now the base case assumption (that’s our perspective too, but the removal of skepticism creates a downside risk for markets to the extent any hiccups emerge ). A Washington Post article around 5am ET contained the somewhat ominous headline “US sends thousands more troops to Mideast” , although that framing is a bit misleading (the troops are on boats that have been traveling to the region for several days, and so this isn’t as incremental as it may seem ); all the tea leaves still suggest Trump is pivoting aggressively away from kinetic escalation . There wasn’t anything major on the economic data or monetary policy fronts since the Tues close. For earnings , the big developments are European luxury , where both Hermes and Kering are getting hit following underwhelming Q1 sales results, and European tech , where ASML and Aixtron rally on back of solid results/guidance.
• How we’re thinking about markets – we continue to feel sanguine about the durability of the current Iran ceasefire (it’s likely the détente gets extended , and the two sides eventually reach some type of a sustainable understanding ) and Q1 earnings have been impressive so far (although it’s still very early in the reporting period). However, our Iran optimism is no longer that far out of the consensus (although there remains a fair degree of skepticism and cynicism), there is still going to be economic fallout from the war even if the fighting permanently ends, and conditions won’t go back to the way they were for a long time (meaning that ~$90 could be a Brent floor into the summer, if not beyond). In addition, the SPX has surged ~10% from its ~6300 trough in pretty much a straight line (the nadir was reached on 3/30), the index is within the 6900-7000 pre-war resistance zone , and technical tailwinds (in terms of positioning and sentiment) have diminished (but people are still pretty negative – a Bo A survey found that fund managers are the most bearish they’ve been since Q2 of last year). Because of this, we’re turning neutral in the near-term .

Iran

• What bulls are saying : media reports suggest the US and Iran remain in communication and are working to narrow their differences , while another face-to-face meeting could occur within days . Trump told Fox’s Maria Bartiromo in an interview airing Wed morning that the war is “very close to being over” and mentioned to ABC News that “ I think you’re going to be watching an amazing two days ahead ” while saying in a NY Post interview that additional peace negotiations “could be happening over the next two days” . It appears that Trump has given up on kinetic escalation as a viable option. A Bloomberg report said Iran could suspend Hormuz shipments for the time being to avoid risking a military confrontation with the US blockade. Europe is putting together a plan that would see it commit military resources to clearing Hormuz of mines once fighting formally ends according to the WSJ . Israel and Lebanon have a long way to go before the two sides reach a deal, but the fact they are even sitting down (as they did on Tuesday) is significant (the US State Dept. issued a statement Tues afternoon and expressed hope that the current dialogue could eventually yield an agreement ).
• What bears are saying : Trump remains stuck in the Middle East , unable to escalate or exit . The bid-ask between Washington and Tehran still seems wide (20 vs. 5 years on enrichment suspension is a big gap), and time is of the essence with the ceasefire set to expire next week. A Washington Post report Wed morning talked about how the Pentagon is moving thousands of additional troops into the Middle East . It’s not just Iran – the Lebanon war continues to rage and resolving that will be even harder than reconciling the nuclear differences between Washington and Tehran. The economic fallout from the war is only just starting to seep into the data , and investors should be prepared for months (at least) of stagflationary pressure (the Mar PPI wasn’t as hot as feared, but headline still accelerated meaningfully vs. Feb to +4% Y/Y).
