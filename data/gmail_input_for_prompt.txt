WHAT'S HAPPENING THIS MORNING:
What’s happening this morning – equities are giving back a bit of the Wed rally as investors sit on the edge of their seats to see whether the fragile ceasefire holds . Incremental news since the US close yesterday has been pretty quiet , and the next major (scheduled) event will be the (tentative) US-Iran meeting set to take place Sat morning in Pakistan (late on Friday in the US). While there were ceasefire violations earlier Wed, it seems like the détente is very much in place so far Thurs – there were no reports of strikes on Iran, and Iran seems to have fired off no drones/missiles of its own , while the foreign ministers of Iran and Saudi Arabia spoke this morning for the first time since the war commenced . The two big flies in the ceasefire ointment are Lebanon (Israel apparently promised Washington it would dial back the intensity of its strikes against Hezbollah for the next two weeks, but this theater is not included in the ceasefire) and Hormuz (where traffic remains depressed and Iran seems to be pushing forward with plans for a toll). In AI , the big news isn’t Meta’s Muse Spark (which we wrote about here on Wed) but instead the new models from Anthropic and Open AI . Anthropic’s warnings about its new model being so powerful that it couldn’t be released right away to the public weighed heavily on software sentiment , and it sounds like Open AI will be adopting a similar approach to its latest model (according to Axios ).

HOW WE'RE THINKING ABOUT MARKETS:
How we’re thinking about markets – there is a lot of skepticism and cynicism when it comes to the cease fire and this, coupled with continued technical imbalances (in terms of lean long positioning and negative sentiment) point to more equity upside in the immediate term (we think the ceasefire will hold , for reasons outlined in a piece published Wed morning; the next major event will probably be the face-to-face talks between US and Iranian officials tentatively scheduled for Friday in Pakistan ). In addition, consumer-linked firms sounded fairly sanguine about the macro landscape (including earnings from DAL and LEVI , and remarks from WMT’s CFO), and this was in reference to a time before the ceasefire took effect . However, since the SPX was struggling with 6900-7000 even before the war , that range is going to be even stronger resistance now . Meanwhile, the inability of Treasuries to rally Wed (and so far Thurs) is a big red flag , and points to the risk of the war’s inflationary effects being larger and more durable than anticipated (the Mar CPI on Fri is expected to show a huge acceleration vs. Feb).

US MACRO:
US macro

• The stagflationary pulse sparked by the ~5-week war is coursing through the global economy , and it’s not clear when (or if) this will dissipate (it might not be fully out of the data until the late summer or fall).
• The Fed will probably (at best) be on the sidelines this year when it comes to the Funds Rate , and the pace of asset purchases is set to slide meaningfully after this month (while the BOE, ECB, and BOJ are all likely to hike rates this year).
• The Fed minutes Wed afternoon contained a lot of hawkish commentary about upside inflation risks, and some central bank officials wanted to warn markets of the risk for potential rate hikes . That said, Fed officials are still inclined to cut rates assuming data unfolds as anticipated (“many participants judged that, in time, it would likely become appropriate to lower the target range for the federal funds rate if inflation were to decline in line with their expectations”).
• US fertility rate fell to a fresh record low in 2025 , hitting 53.1 births per 1000 women of childbearing age (down from 53.8 in 2024) ( NYT )

Following a NYT report back in Mar about how Meta’s new model was underperforming on internal tests , investors were happy to see Muse Spark formally unveiled , and that it seemed to do decently on benchmarks vs. peers (according to the WSJ , Meta claimed Muse Spark significantly outperformed Grok and did better than Gemini on certain tests while being competitive against Chat GPT and Claude on others ). All that said, there remain a lot of questions about why Meta is pouring so much money into AI given that it has fairly little to show for it (Meta’s various AI initiatives and products lag far behind other frontier firms like Anthropic, Open AI, and Google in terms of market and mindshare , and Muse Spark might, at best, be at parity with those other firms technically , all of whom are rushing to release updated products). It’s a testament to the strength of Meta’s core business that mgmt. can expend so many resources on AI without having to constantly raise fresh capital (a luxury x AI, Open AI, and Anthropic don’t have), but that still doesn’t mean it’s a good idea from an ROI perspective (so far, Apple’s approach to AI, whereby it’s expending minimal cash to repurpose Gemini and earning fees via the App Store on the whole industry, seems like the wisest from a free cash flow perspective).

Hello there,

Carmel is away, so this week’s newsletter is coming to you from Frankfurt, where I cover European economics and the European Central Bank.

This week’s yarn is about Hungary, my native land, which is holding elections on Sunday in possibly the most consequential vote until the U.S. midterms in November.

The outcome is likely to shape Russia’s war in Ukraine, impact thorny relations between the U.S. and the European Union and influence Europe’s long-stalled internal reform process.

Viktor Orbán, the incumbent prime minister, has been a thorn in the EU’s side for 16 tumultuous years, but is now at risk of losing power .

Supporters say he is a Christian patriot, who fights against the EU’s liberal “wokeism” and takes a pragmatic foreign policy stance, keeping friendly ties with like-minded politicians such as U.S. President Donald Trump, Russian President Vladimir Putin and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu.

Critics say he’s a corrupt, far-right nationalist, who has systematically dismantled democratic institutions and serves foreign interests from within the EU.
He seemed untouchable for years but allegations of corruption, which he denies, damaging leaks that laid bare the prevalence of Russian influence and lacklustre economic growth have now pushed Orbán to the brink of defeat.

Most polls show political newcomer Peter Magyar, who has built an opposition from scratch in just two years, well ahead, possibly on course for a two-thirds majority, and Polymarket puts his chances of victory at about 70%.

But Hungarian polls are notoriously inaccurate and Orbán has rewritten election laws to favour his party, redrawing electoral districts in the process. He controls a vast media empire, uses state funds to spread his message - and this week enlisted U.S. Vice President JD Vance for a campaign stop.
